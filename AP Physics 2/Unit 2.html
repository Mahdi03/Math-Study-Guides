Thermodynamics:

  • This is the study of the motion of heat, but heat is just another form of energy, so essentially it's just the study of how energy moves through matter
  • In this unit, matter is going to be made up of a lot of tiny particles that are constantly bumping and colliding into each other
  • In ideal gasses, these particle collisions are held to be elastic, meaning that no energy is lost within the collisions
    • Maxwell-Boltzmann Distribution:

  • Remember that the Average Kinetic Energy of the particles is directly related to the temperature
  • If you know the mass of the particles, you can find the velocity distribution of the same particles from this Kinetic Energy distribution

  • Whenever there is an energy difference, heat will transfer from the hotter areas to the colder areas, this is given by the 2nd Law of Thermodynamics
  • This heat transfer occurs until the two items or mediums have reached a thermal equilibrium, remember that in thermal equilibrium there is the most randomness in the assortment of particles, this is also known as entropy.
  • There are three types of heat transfer:
    1. Conduction - heat transfer through physical conduct, this happens when particles of a hotter substance transfer their mechanical energy to particles of a colder substance through collisions where momentum is conserved, and with higher temperatures, an object's particles are moving faster.

    2. Convection - heat transfer through a fluid (remember that air is also a fluid!), warmer fluids are less dense than colder fluids so they rise and the colder fluids sink (remember "hot air rises" from hurricanes)
    3. Radiation - heat transfer through electromagnetic waves being emitted or absorbed (in fact, everything emits some sort of radiation based on its temperature, and they absorb radiation based on their albedo)

    The First Law of Thermodynamics:
  • This law is in a similar essence to that of the conservation of energy:
    ΔU = Q + W
    where ΔU is the total internal energy of the gas which is directly related to the temperature, Q is the heat transferred into the gas (if heat leaves the gas, then Q is negative), and W is work done on the gas. If the gas is compressed, W is positive. But if the gas does work on an environment, W is negative.
  • We can look at Pressure v. Volume graphs (PV-graphs) to see these trends
  • In a PV-graph, the area under the curve(s) is work, which is given by the formula:
    • W = −PΔV
  • If the overall area under the curve is positive, then the net work done BY the gas is also positive
  • When change in volume (ΔV = 0), work is done on the system, therefore work is negative since the gas is expanding
  • Looking at this diagram:
