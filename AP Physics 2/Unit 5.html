    • Electrostatic Charge:

  • Static charge exists in every day aspects of life, when you rub your feet against the carpet and then shock someone, or just simply trying to get out of your car on a hot and dry day. There are three ways to transfer charge:
    1. Friction:
      • Different objects have different electronegativities, or a want for electrons. When materials are rubbed together, electrons from one object could move to the other, charging one object positively in the absence of electrons on the surface, and charging the other negatively in the excess of electrons on the surface.
      • The charge transferred between the two objects must be the same due to the Conservation of Charge, so if one object gets −5mC, the other one gains a charge of +5mC.
    2. Conduction:
      • When charge is built-up, touching the charged object with something of a different charge will cause the charges to transfer and spread evenly, reaching somewhat of an equilibrium of charge. If the object is a conductor, then charge will spread evenly across the conductor as well.
      • If an object is an insulator, charge has a hard time getting through, and if the object is a conductor (like metals), charge can easily flow through.
    3. Induction:
      • This third method only works on conductors as it requires the charge to separate through an object before the object is separated, and with it, the charges.
      • While this method requires the usage of an already charged object to be held close to the object to-be-charged, there are two ways to do it:
        1. The first way involves using a separable conductor and a charged object. You take the charged object a put it close enough to the conductor to polarize the conductor, but not let it touch because touching would transfer the charge through conduction. Then when the conductor has been polarized, separate that conductor and then take away the initially charged object.
        2. The second method is to again take a conductor and polarize it with a charged object, but this time connect it to ground so that either the excess electrons go into ground (if the original charged object is negatively charged), or the ground supplies excess electrons (if the original charged object is positively charged).

    Electric Potential Energy and Potential:

  • We say that when two charges are infinitely apart, the electric potential energy between them is 0. Otherwise, it is positive when like charges repel, and negative when opposite charges attract.   • Unlike Electric Force, Electric Potential Energy is a scalar, so the number has no directional meaning. In a system of charges, finding the Electric Potential Energy is just as easy as adding up all the Electric Potential Energies between each charge.
  • Analogous to the relationship between Electric Force and Electric Fields, Electric Potential Energy and Electric Potential share the same relationship of being off by a charge. Electric Potential tells us how much Electric Potential Energy there would be at a location in space if there were to be a charge there, just like how Electric Fields tell us how much Electric Force would be applied to a charge at a certain point in space.
