Magnetic Fields:

  • Magnetism is the most complex force we've seen so far. It is always the result of moving/charged particles, which would mean that current moving through a wire must induce magnetism.
  • In a random material, the spin of electrons is randomized and pointed in random directions, so overall the electron spins cancel out. But in ferromagnetic materials (like iron), their electron spins can be aligned to cause a net magnetic field. Bar magnets have ferromagnetism.
  • Magnetic fields are measured in Teslas (T), and 1T is really powerful, most of our problems will deal with magnetic fields in the mT to μT range.
  • In a uniform magnetic field, electrons move in circular motion. This happens because with centripetal motion, the force is perpendicular to the direction of motion (the Force vector and the velocity vector are orthogonal).
  • Forces applied to moving charge by a magnetic field obeys the right-hand rule, where your thumb is the direction of velocity, and your four fingers point in the direction of the magnetic field. If the charge is negative, the force is in the direction of your back-hand. If the charge is positive, it is in the direction of the palm.
  • We've seen from previous physics formulas that dot products and cross products relate to vectors. In scenarios where we only care about the parallel components of vectors, we employ the dot product (see the formula for work). And for scenarios where we only care about the perpendicular components of vectors, we employ the cross product. Remember that the result of a cross-product of two vectors is always a vector that is orthogonal to the first two vectors. This is why we need 3-D representations of space on 2-D papers for this unit.
  • We give the magnetic force on a motionary point-charge to be:
    FM = q v ⨯ B || FM|| = ||q v|| || B|| θ
    where q is the charge of the particle, v is the velocity, and B gives the magnetic field. For the second equation we can apply the properties of the vector cross-product to allow us to deal with the magnitudes of the vectors and the angle in between them.
  • We can find the force of magnetism exerted by a straight wire using a very similar formula:
    FM = I l ⨯ B || FM|| = ||I l|| || B|| θ
    where I is the current, l is the length vector, and once again B is the magnetic field.
  • Magnets in general try to line up with their external magnetic field, we know how with charges, negative and positive attract, North and South poles of magnets attract.
  • Since current running through a wire also exerts a magnetic field, the magnetic field calculated at a distance r from a straight wire is given by the Biot-Savart Law:

    • Electromagnetic Induction:

  • Traffic lights use loops that function as circuits with little currents that run through them. This current induces a magnetic field.
  • Since the car is made of ferromagnetic materials, the car itself becomes magnetized, and in turn, the magnetized car now adds to the magnetic field. With an increased magnetic field, more current is induced in the loop, and the sensor accounts for the change in current.
  • From this we see that just how an electric circuit induces a magnetic field, a magnetic field can also induce a current. We call this Electromagnetic Induction and it is the converse of a changing electric field to cause magnetism (first part of this unit).
  • We use Electromagnetic Induction to generate power in most ways (except solar panels). We convert mechanical energy in wind and water turbines to electric energy through the usage of changing magnetic fields.
  • We measure the amount of magnetic field B passing through an area A as magnetic flux, denoted by ΦB.
  • We say that the area is a vector A because we need a way to multiply with the magnetic field vector B, and we denote the area vector to be perpendicular to the plane of the area (like a cross product, it will stick out of the plane).
  • Since A and B run parallel (see image above), we only care about the parallel parts of the vectors, so we would employ a dot product in this place. Magnetic Flux is given by
    ΦB = A ⋅ B ΦB = ||A|| ||B|| θ
  • From this formula we can see that the units for magnetic flux would be in tesla⋅meters2 (T⋅m2).
    • Faraday's Law:
  • The reason we want to measure magnetic flux in the first place is to see how the change in the magnetic field vector B changes the current in the circuit.
  • This is given by Faraday's Law, that in fact, the rate of change in the magnetic flux directly correleates to the induced current. Faraday's Law is in terms of ε, which is just an electromotive force (emf). Emf is just the archaic term for the induced voltage, so we can hold ε = V to be true.

  • One thing to keep in mind is that some places add a coefficient N to the mix, this just signifies that in the case of a coil with multiple turns, each turn counts as a loop so you would multiply by the number of loops.
  • From the voltage, if we know the resistance in the wire, we can calculate the current using Ohm's Law.
    • Lenz's Law:
  • The reason there is a (−) sign as a coefficient of the equation is actually given by another law that is sound with other fundamental forces.
  • Lenz's Law states that induced current wants to oppose the direction of change in magnetic flux, which is just nature's way of keeping equilibrium. If you're in chemistry, this is similar to Le Chatelier's Principle in equilibrium reactions. Also think of Hooke's Law, the spring force opposes the direction of movement in an effort to bring the spring back to equilibrium.
  • From Lenz's Law we can actually create an alternating current (AC).
  • The way we do this is by taking a loop and spinning it using mechanical energy.

    • Motional emf:
  • Although this method is rarely used since the energy cannot be harnessed from it, it is still possible to create a voltage difference without a loop/circuit.
  • When you move a straight wire through a magnetic field, the electrons in the wire will move in the opposite direction as the protons based on the right-hand rule of charged particles in a magnetic field. As the charged particles separate, the wire itself is polarized, and the separation causes a voltage difference we can also quantify with emf ε:

  • However, there is no way to really harness the voltage difference without a circuit.
