Photoelectron Spectroscopy:

  • The energies of electrons in a given shell can be measured experimentally with photoelectron spectroscopy. We can see these values on a PhotoElectron Spectroscopy (PES) chart, which we can use to match up to the electron configuration and identify which element is in question.

Periodic Trends:

  • Quick summary:
    • Atomic Radius Increases: Ionization Energy Increases: Electronegativity Increases:
  • By Coulomb's Law, we can say that the closer an electron is to the nucleus, the more strongly it is attracted. And we can also deduce that the more protons in a nucleus, the more strongly an electron is attracted.
  • If there are electrons between a valence electron and the nucleus, the valence electrons will be less attracted to the nucleus, this is known as the Shielding effect, because the valence electrons are shielded from the pull of the nucleus.
  • Electron shells are most stable when they are completely full.
    • Atomic Radius:
  • Decreases moving left to right - As you go across a period, protons are added to the nucleus, so valence electrons are more strongly attracted to the nucleus, thus shrinking the size of the atom.
  • Increases moving down a group - As you go down a group, more electron shells are added, which adds distance to how far electrons are from the nucleus
    • Ionic Radius:
  • Cations are smaller than Anions because as you remove an electron to form a cation, a shell is removed, and electron-electron repulsion decreases, thus making cations smaller. And as you add an electron to form an anion, electron-electron repulsion increases causing electrons to drift further apart, making anions bigger.
    • Ionization Energy:
  • Ionization energy is the energy needed to remove an electron: X + IE → +
  • Increases going left to right - As you go across a period, protons are added to the nucleus, the electrons are more attracted to the center, requiring more energy to remove an electron
  • Decreases going down - As you go down a group, shells of electrons are added to the nucleus, making electrons easier to remove as they are less attracted to the center
  • The more and more times you remove an electron, the Ionization Energy increases as a result of there being less electron-electron repulsion and being closer to the nucleus. This is the the 2nd Ionization Energy is higher than the 1st.
  • Exceptions:
    • Group 16 has lower IE than group 15 (switch 15&16)
    • Group 12 has higher IE than group 13 (switch 13&12)
    Electronegativity:
  • Electronegativity is the ability of an atom to attract shared electrons towards itself. F, O, N, and Cl are 4 most electronegative elements.
  • It has the same trend as ionization energy:
  • Increases going left to right - The smaller an atom, the more effectively its nuclear charge will be felt past its outermost energy level, and the more likely it will be to attract other electrons
  • Decreases going down - The closer an element is to having a full energy level, the more likely it is to attract electrons to complete the energy level
  • Exceptions:
    • Group 15 has higher electron affinity than group 16 (switch 15&14)
  • The equation of electronegativity is just the reverse of that in ionization energy, so technically by Hess's Law (discussed further on), the ΔH's would be opposite since all you do is flip the equation.

  • The likelihood that two elements will form a chemical bond is determined by the interactions between the valence electrons and nuclei of elements.
