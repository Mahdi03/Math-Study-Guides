Oxidation-Reduction (Redox) Reactions:

  • Abbreviated as redox reactions, these reactions involve the transfer of electrons.
    • Oxidation States:
  • In these kinds of reactions we assign "oxidation states" that are numbers to keep track of how many electrons are in each element in chemical compounds
  • One important difference to note is that oxidation numbers are NOT the same as charges, with charges we would write but with oxidation states we would write
  • With a covalent bond between two identical atoms, we split the number in half
  • In compounds where electrons are unequally shared, all the electrons go towards the more electronegative atom, so in H2O, O will have an excess 2 electrons and H each will have no electrons. So
  • For covalent compounds, these numbers represent the would-be charges, whereas for the ionic compounds, these ARE the charges.
    • Type: Oxidation State: Examples:
    Atom as element 0 Na, O2, Hg
    Monatomic ion Charge of ion Na^+, Cl^-
    Fluorine in compound −1 HF, PF3
    Oxygen in compound −2 H2O, CO2
    Oxygen in peroxide ( ) −1 H2O2
    Hydrogen in covalent compound +1 HCl, NH3
    • Oxidation: increase in oxidation state → loss of electrons
    • Reduction: decrease in oxidation state → gain of electrons
    • Oxidizing Agent: electron acceptor
    • Reducing Agent: electron donor
    Balancing Redox Reactions:
  • In these kinds of reactions, we can check to see if it is a redox reaction by looking at oxidation states and seeing electrons being gained or lost from one side of the equation to the other.
  • In a redox reaction, the total electrons gained and lost must be equal.
  • In order to balance a reaction using Oxidation-Reduction:
    1. Split up the equation based on elements and their oxidation states
    2. Isolate the elements that change oxidation and equalize electrons gained/lost—add these coefficients only to the oxidizing and reducing agents
    3. Balance the rest of the equation normally and add states of matter
