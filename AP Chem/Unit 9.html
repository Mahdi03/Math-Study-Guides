Entropy:

  • Entropy, represented by S, describes the randomness and disorder of a system. 0 entropy is defined as a solid crystal at 0 K. Standard entropies are calculated at 25°C.
  • Standard entropy (of a chemical reaction; \(n_p, n_r\) represent moles of products and reactants respectively):
    • \(\Delta S^{\circ}_{reaction} = \sum n_p S^{\circ}_{products} - \sum n_r S^{\circ}_{reactants}\)
  • The second law of thermodynamics states: in any spontaneous process there is always an increase in the entropy of the universe. Thus, entropy is not conserverd.
    • \(\Delta S_{univ} = \Delta S_{sys} + \Delta S_{surr}\)
  • If \(\Delta S_{univ}\) is positive, entropy increases and the process it decribes is spontaneous in the direction written. If \(\Delta S_{univ}\) is negative, then the process is spontaneous in the opposite direciton. If \(\Delta S_{univ}\) is 0, then the process will not occur spontaneously and the system is at equilibrium.
  • The sign of entropy changes in the surroundings are primarily determined by heat flow: exothermic processes increase surrounding entropy and endothermic is the opposite. The magnitude of surrounding entropy depends on temperature.
    • \(\Delta S_{surr} = -\dfrac{\Delta H}{T}\)
  • Entropy will increase as volume increases and increase as pressure decreases.

Gibb's Free Energy:

  • Gibb's free energy, G, is the measure of whether or not a process will proceed without input of outside energy. A process that proceeds without outside energy is called thermodynamically favored while the opposite is unfavored (also called spontaneous and nonspontaneous, respectively).
    • Free Energy Chance (ΔG°):
    \(\Delta G^{\circ}_{reaction} = \sum n_p G^{\circ}_{products} - \sum n_r G^{\circ}_{reactants}\)
  • If ΔG° is negative, reaction is thermodynamically favored and will proceed spontaneously.
  • If ΔG° is positive, the reaction is thermodynamically unfavored and the opposite reaction will proceed spontaneously.
  • If ΔG° is 0, the reaction is at equilibrium.
    • ΔG, ΔH, and ΔS:
  • Nature likes to move toward low energy and high disorder (entropy), so thermodynamically-favored processes result in decreasing enthalpy or increasing entropy or both. The following equation links these energies together:
    • \(\Delta G^{\circ} = \Delta H^{\circ} - T\Delta S^{\circ}\)
  • This formula can be derived from the formula for the entropy of the universe:

    • \(\Delta S_{univ} = \Delta S_{surr} + \Delta S_{sys}\)

    \(\Delta S_{univ} = -\dfrac{\Delta G}{T} \quad \Delta S_{surr} = -\dfrac{\Delta H}{T}\)

    \(-\dfrac{\Delta G}{T} = -\dfrac{\Delta H}{T} + \Delta S_{(sys)}\)

    \(\Delta G = \Delta H - T\Delta S\)

  • Here's an example:

    \(\ce{CO(g) + H2O(g) -> CO2(g) + H2(g) \quad \Delta H^{\circ} = -41.2 kJ/mol \quad \Delta S^{\circ} = -135 J/mol\cdot K}\)

    1. Is the above reaction thermodynamically favorable at 25°C?
    2. At what temperature would the reaction no longer be thermodynamically favored? Assume the enthalpy and entropy values are independent of temperature.
      3. Show Solution
    1. We can use our equation above to plug in our values.
      2. \(\ce{\Delta G^{\circ} = (-41.2 kJ/mol) - (298 K)(-0.135 kJ/mol\cdot K)}\)
      \(\quad \ce{\Delta G^{\circ} = -0.97 kJ/mol}\)
    2. Set the gibbs energy to 0 and then solve.
      3. \(\quad \ce{0=(-41.2 kJ/mol) - (T)(-0.135 kJ/mol\cdot K)}\)
      \(\quad T = 305 K\), thus its dynamically unfavorable if it is above that temperature.
    ΔG° and Phase Changes:
  • Since phase changes are not chemical reactions, their free energy is 0. So, we can determine a substance's melting or boiling points.
    • Standard Free Energy Change and the Equilibrium Constant:
  • Free energy can be calculated if you know Q or K of a specific reaction. \(\Delta G^{\circ}\) represents the free energy change when there is 1 atm while the other represents free energy change for a specified pressure. \(R = \ce{8.3145 J/K \cdot mol}\)
    • At Equilibrium: Not At Equilibrium:
    \(\Delta G = \Delta G^{\circ} + R T \ln Q\) \(\Delta G^{\circ} = -R T \ln K\)

Redox Reactions:

    Half-Reactions:
    • A half-reaction is composed of a molecule that is either oxidized or reduced during a specific reaction. They can be used to balance redox reactions.
    • Example: The following equation is not balanced.
      \(\ce{Ce^4+(aq) + Sn^2+(aq) -> Ce^3+(aq) + Sn^4+(aq)}\)
      Separate the oxidized and reduces species into their own half-reactions, and balance them so electrons cancel:
      Reduced: \(\ce{2Ce^4+(aq) + 2e- -> 2Ce^3+(aq)}\)
      Oxidized: \(\ce{Sn^2+(aq) -> Sn^4+(aq) + 2e-}\)
      Sum of half-reactions:
      \(\ce{2Ce^4+(aq) + Sn^2+(aq) + 2e- -> 2Ce^3+(aq) + Sn^4+(aq) + 2e-}\)
      \(\ce{2Ce^4+(aq) + Sn^2+(aq) -> 2Ce^3+(aq) + Sn^4+(aq)}\)
    Redox Reactions Occurring in an Acidic Solutions:
    • In these types of redox reactions, water molecules, protons, or hydroxide ions may be needed to balance the equations. Here is how to balance such equations:
      1. Write separate equatiosn for the oxidation and reduction half-reactions.
      2. For each half-reaciton,
        1. balance all the elements except hydrogen and oxygen.
        2. balance oxygen using \(\ce{H2O}\)
        3. balance hydrogen using \(\ce{H+}\)
        4. balance charge using electrons
      3. If necessary, multiply one or both half-reactions by an integer to equalize the number of electrons transferred.
      4. Add the half-reactions, and cancel identical species.
    • Example: The following equation is not balanced.
      \(\ce{Cu(s) + NO3-(aq) -> Cu^2+(aq) NO2(g)}\)
      Write the half-reactions,
      Oxidation: \(\ce{Cu(s) -> Cu^2+(aq)}\)
      Reduction: \(\ce{NO3-(aq) -> NO2(g)}\)
      Now, balance each half reaction,
      Oxidation: \(\ce{Cu(s) -> Cu^2+(aq) + 2e-}\)
      Reduction:
      \(\ce{NO3-(aq) -> NO2(g) + H2O(l)}\) (Notice that the Oxygens are now balanced)
      \(\ce{NO3-(aq) + 2H+(aq) -> NO2(g) + H2O(l)}\) (Now the hydrogens are balanced)
      \(\ce{NO3-(aq) + 2H+(aq) + e- -> NO2(g) + H2O(l)}\) (The net charge on both sides is now 0)
      Now we must equalize the number of electrons transferred:
      Reduction: \(\ce{2NO3-(aq) + 4H+(aq) + 2e- -> 2NO2(g) + 2H2O(l)}\)
      Summing both reactions yields us:
      \(\ce{Cu(s) + 2NO3-(aq) + 4H+(aq) -> Cu^2+(aq) + 2NO2(g) + 2H2O(l)}\)

    • Note: when dealing with acidic solutions use \(\ce{H+}\) and with basic solutions its more complicated:
    Redox Reactions Occurring in an Acidic Solutions:
      1. Use the half-reaction method as in acidic solutions (we are assuming that hydrogen ions are present).
      2. To both sides of the equation affected, add a number of hydroxide ions that is equal to the number of hydrogen ions. This will eliminate the hydrogen ions by forming water.
      3. Form water on the side containing both hydrogen and hydroxide ions, and eliminate the number of water molecules that appear on both sides of the equation.
      4. Check that elements and charges are balanced.
    • Example: The following reaction is not balanced.
      \(\ce{Ag(s) + CN-(aq) + O2(g) ->[Base] Ag(CN)2-(aq)}\)
      Oxidation:
      \(\ce{CN-(aq) + Ag(s) -> Ag(CN)2-(aq)}\)
      \(\ce{2CN-(aq) + Ag(s) -> Ag(CN)2-(aq) + e-}\)
      Reduction:
      \(\ce{O2(g) ->}\)
      \(\ce{O2(g) -> 2H2O(l)}\)
      \(\ce{O2(g) + 4H+(aq) -> 2H2O(l)}\)
      \(\ce{4e- + O2(g) + 4H+(aq) -> 2H2O(l)}\)
      After multiplying the oxidation half-reaction by 4, the complete, balanced reaciton is:
      \(\ce{8CN-(aq) + 4Ag(s) + O2(g) +} \underbrace{\ce{4H+(aq) + 4OH-(aq)}}_{\ce{4H2O(l)}} \ce{-> 4Ag(CN)2-(aq) + 2H2O(l) + 4OH-(aq)}\)
      After eliminating the water molecules from both sides:
      \(\ce{8CN-(aq) + 4Ag(s) + O2(g) + 2H2O(l) -> 4Ag(CN)2-(aq) + 4OH-(aq)}\)

Galvanic Cells:

  • A favored redox reaction is used to generate a flow of current (the flow of electrons). As shown in the figure below, two half-reactions take place in separate chambers, and electrons are released by the oxidation reaction pass through the wire to the reduction reaction's chamber. Oxidation takes place at the electrode called the anode and reduction takes place at the cathode.
  • The salt bridge maintains electrical neutrality in the cell. At the cathode, reduction occurs causing the solution to become less positively charged, so positive cations from the salt bridge solution flow into the half-cell. The opposite happens at the anode.
  • For electrochemical cells, we use line notation and in this notation the anode components are listed on the left and the cathode components are listed on the right. As shown above, phase difference is indicated by a single line. The substance constituting the anode is on the far left and the substance constituting the cathode is on the far right. Double lines separate the anode and cathode.
  • For an electrochemical cell containing ions (an aqueous solution) instead of a solid, platinum (a non-reactive, conducting metal) can be used to serve as an electrode. Thus, the line notation will contain commas separating the components of the half-reaction and a line separating those from platinum. Here is an electrochemical cell using two non-solid substances:
    \(\ce{Pt(s)|ClO3-(aq), ClO4-(aq), H+(aq)||H+(aq), MnO4-(aq), Mn2+(aq)|Pt(s)}\)
    • Reduction Potential:
  • The driving force on electrons in a ce ll is called the cell potential (\(E_{cell}\)) and is measured in volts (a volt is defined as 1 joule of work per coulomb of charge transferred). \(E^{\circ}\) has a superscript indicating that standard states are used (1 atm gases and 1 M solutes).
  • Each half-reaction in a redox reaction is assigned a half-cell potential. The half-reaction of \(\ce{2H+ + 2e- -> H2}\) is defined to have a half-cell potential of 0 V. Convention: potentials of half-reactions are given as reduction processes (so you would see an electron being in the reactant side). Because the oxidation process is written as a reduction process, the cell potential is given by:
    \(E^{\circ}_{cell} = E^{\circ}_{cathode} - E^{\circ}_{anode}\)
  • Note: although half-reactions may not have equal amounts of electrons if they are unbalanced, standard reduction potential is an intensive property so therefore it does not depend on how many times the reaction occurs. (Do not multiply the potential by any integer when balancing a redox reaction). Also, the sign of the cell potential flips when the reaction is flipped.
  • A fast way to determine which half-reaction should go first is to see which order of half-cell potentials will yield a positive cell potiental. Only a positive one will create a spontaneous reaction.

Electrolytic Cells:

  • An electrolytic cell uses an outside source of voltage to force an unfavored redox reaction to take place. Most occur in aqueous solutions and either ions or the water molecules can be reduced oxidized.
  • Example:
    For a reaction involving an aqueous solution of nickel (II) chloride in water, we must determine which substance is reduced and which is oxidized.
    \(\ce{Ni^2+ + 2e- -> Ni(s) \quad E^{\circ} = -0.25 V}\)
    \(\ce{2H2O(l) + 2e- -> H2O(g) + 2OH- \quad E^{\circ} = -0.80 V}\)
    Since the half-reaction involving nickel has a higher voltage, it is more favored. It will occur over the one involving water.
    \(\ce{2Cl- -> Cl2(g) + 2e- \quad E^{\circ} = -1.36 V}\)
    \(\ce{2H2O(l) -> O2(g) + 4H+ + 4e- \quad E^{\circ} = -1.23 V}\)
    Since the half-reaction for water has a higher voltage, it is more favored.
    Thus, the complete redox reaction is:
    \(\ce{2Ni^2+ + 2H2O(l) -> 2Ni(s) + O2(g) + 4H+}\)
    \(E = -0.25 V + -1.24 V = -1.49 V\)
  • The total cell potential in an electrolytic cell is always negative as an outside source (like a battery) is needed to power the reaction. They usually use two non-reactive metal bars as electrodes since the components are aqueous or water.

Electroplating

  • Electroplating refers how metal ions will congregate on the surface of the cathode or where the reduced half-reaction takes place. This process is usually implemented in an electrolytic cell.
  • The most common problem type asks you to find how much a component "plates out" or congregates on an electrode (usually in grams). First, the idea of current is needed to find this amount:
    \(I = \dfrac{q}{t}\)
    \(I\) = current (amperes, A)
    \(q\) = charge (coulombs, C)
    \(t\) = time (seconds, s)
  • Faraday's constant will also be needed:
    \(F = 96485 \frac{C}{mol}\)
    It represents the charge of 1 mole of electrons.
  • Using those equations, you can figure out how many moles of electrons are produced or needed in a reaction with a given current and time. Also, utilizing stoichiometry (not forgetting the coefficient on the electron), you can find the mass of any redox component.

Voltage and Favorability

  • For standard conditions,
    \(\Delta G^{\circ} = -nFE^{\circ}\)
    Basically, the maximum cell potential is directly proportional to the free energy difference between reactants and products in the cell. \(n\) represents the number of moles of electrons exchanged in the reaction.
  • Concentration: by Le Châtelier's principle, an increase in the concentration of the reactants will favor the favor reaction and thus increase flow of electrons. Thus, cell potential will increase. An increase in concentration of the product will oppose the forward reaction and thus decrease the cell potential.
  • Because of concentration, it is possible to produce a positive voltage with the same component in two different half-cells at different concentrations (a concentration cell). The half-cell with less concentration will donate electrons to the half-cell with more concentration in order to increase its concentration of ions and decrease the half-cell's.
    • THe Nernst Equation:
  • Using the equation: \(\Delta G = \Delta G^{\circ} + RT \ln (Q)\), we can use the equation above to find a relationship for voltage and concentration.
    \(E = E^{\circ} - \dfrac{RT}{nF} \ln (Q)\)
  • The cell potential calcuated with this equation is the maximum potential before any current flows. Thus, as current does flow, concentrations change and so an equilibrium will be reached. The cell will spontaneously discharge until it reaches equilibrium.
    \(Q = K \quad \text{and} \quad E_{cell} = 0\)
    The battery becomes dead, the components have the same free energy and the cell no longer has the ability to do work.
