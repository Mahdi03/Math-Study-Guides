Heat and Temperature:

  • Energy is the capacity to do work or to produce heat. The first law of thermodynamics states that energy can never be created or destroyed.
  • Kinetic energy of an object is the energy due to the motion of the object, given by the equation:
    \(\text{KE} = \frac{1}{2} mv^2\)
  • Heat represents energy flow between two substances at different temperatures. Temperature represents the average amount of kinetic energy a substance has.
  • In an exothermic process, energy is transferred from the system to its surroundings. In an endothermic process, energy is transferred from the surroundings into the process. Both physical and chemical changes can be classified as exo or enderthermic.

Enthalpy:

  • Enthalpy of a substance is the measure of energy that is release or absorbed by the substance when bonds are broken and formed during a reaction. A change of enthalpy is equal to the energy flow as heat:
    \(\Delta H = H_{products} - H_{reactants}\)
  • When the change in enthalpy is positive, heat will be absorbed by the system and it is endothermic. When it is negative, there is a generation of heat and the reaction is exothermic.

Enthalpy of Formation \(\Delta H^{\circ}_f\):

  • The standard enthalpy of formation \(\Delta H^{\circ}_f\) of a compound is defined as the change in enthalpy that accompanies the formation of 1 mole of a ocmpound from its elements with all substances in their standard states.
  • When ever you see a degree symbol on any thermodynamic function, it indicates that the process has been carried out under standard conditions.
  • Standard States:
    • Gas: pressure of 1 atm
    • Pure substance in liquid or solid form: standard state is pure liquid or solid.
    • Solution: concentration of exactly 1M
  • Enthalpy of formation is ALWAYS given per 1 mole of product:
    $$\ce{C(s) + 2H2(g) + 1/2O2(g) -> CH3OH(l)} \quad \Delta H^{\circ}_f = -239 kJ/mol$$
  • \(\Delta H^{\circ}_f\) for elements is defined to be 0. If \(\Delta H^{\circ}_f < 0\) for a compound, its products are more stable than its reactants. If \(\Delta H^{\circ}_f > 0\), the converse is true.
  • The enthalpy change for a given reaction is given by:
    \(\Delta H^{\circ}_{reaction} = \sum n_p \Delta H^{\circ}_f \text{(products)} - \sum n_r \Delta H^{\circ}_f \text{(reactants)}\)
    The terms containing n represent the moles of each molecule in the reaction. Elements are not included because they have no change in form.
  • Combustion reactions involve hydrocarbon that is formed into carbon dioxide and water with an excess amount of oxygen as a reactant. The change in enthalpy is given with respect to one mole of the hydrocarbon. In order to solve one of these problems, balance the reaction and use stoichiometry to convert a given mass of hydrocarbon to its enthalpy of combustion with the given enthalpy of formation.

Bond Energy:

  • Bond energy is the energy required to break a bond. Bonding is endothermic so the bond enregy is always positive.
  • The change of enthalpy can be calculated using bond energies:
    \(\Delta H^{\circ} = \sum \text{Bond energies of bonds broken (reactants)} - \sum \text{Bond energies of bonds formed (products)}\)

Hess's Law:

  • The law states that if a reaction can be described as a series of steps, then \(\Delta H\) for the overall reaction is the sum of all the \(\Delta H\) values for each step.
  • Three rules for manupulating equations in enthalpy calculations:
    1. If you flip the reaction, flip the sign of the enthalpy value.
    2. If you multiply or divide an equation by an integer, also multiply/divide the enthalpy value by the same integer
    3. If many equations, when summed up, create a new equation, the sum of their enthalpy values will be the enthalpy value of the new equation.

Enthalpy of Solution:

  • When an ionic substance dissolves in water, heat is emitted or absorbed. The bond between the cation and anion breaks, requiring energy, but energy is also released when new attractions to water molecules forms.
  • Dissolving can be separated into three steps:
    1. The first first process breaks the inframolecular forces holding the solute (NaCl) together. It can be represented with \(\Delta H_1 > 0\). Its endothermic: $$\ce{NaCl(s) ->[energy in] Na+(g) + Cl-(g)}$$
    2. The next step occurs when the intermolecular forces holding the solvent (water) together break up. It can be represented with \(\Delta H_2 > 0\). $$\ce{H2O(l) ->[energy in] H2O(g)}$$
    3. The third step combines the two together. Bonds are not being formed, but energy is still released so \(\Delta H_3 < 0\). $$\ce{Na+(g) + Cl-(g) + H2O(g) ->[energy out] Na+(aq) + Cl-(aq)}$$
  • Energy values from step 2 and 3 can be combined and that sum is called the hydration energy. That value is always negative since step 3 has a larger magnitude of energy than step 2. Hydration energy increases with Coulombic energy (use the formula to which ions will have a greater energy similar to lattice energy).
  • The sum of all the steps, \(\Delta H_{solution} = \Delta H_1 + \Delta H_2 + \Delta H_3\), is the enthalpy of solution for that compount. If hydration energy exceeds the lattice energy, the enthalpy is negative. If the converse it true, then enthalpy is positive. A solution is called an ideal solution if \(\Delta H_{solution} = 0\), else it is a non-ideal solution.

Phase Change:

    Naming the Phase Changes
    • Vapor pressure is the pressure exerted by liquid or solid molecules as they escape from the surface. When temperature increases in a substance, the vapor pressure will increase. When that vapor pressure becomes equal to the surroudning atmospheric pressure, the liquid boils.
    • Names for phase changes:
      • Melting - Solid to Liquid
      • Freeze - Liquid to Solid
      • Vaporization - Liquid to Gas
      • Condensation - Gas to Liquid
      • Sublimation - Solid to Gas
      • Deposition - Gas to Solid
    Heat of Fusion:
    • The heat of fusion or enthalpy of fusion is the energy required to melt a solid. It's the energy to overcome inframolecular forces holding the solid together. It is also the heat given off by a substance when it freezes (the sign is just flipped).
    Heat of Vaporization:
    • Heat of vaporization or enthalpy of vaporization is the energy needed to turn a liquid into a gas. Its opposite is the heat given off by a substance when it condenses.
    • As heat is added to a substance, the temperature can increase or the substance can change phases, but NOT both at the same time. So, when changing phases, the temperature is constant.

Calorimetry:

  • The heat capacity, C, of a substance is measured by:
    \(C = \dfrac{\text{heat absorbed}}{\text{increase in temperature}}\)
  • Heat capacity is per gram, so it is important to not that the specific heat capacity is given in \(\ce{J/C\cdot g}\) or in \(\ce{J/K \cdot g}\). Moles can also be used instead of grams.
  • Calorimetry is the measurement of heat changes during a chemical reaction. Under constant pressure, it can help find the enthalpy for that reaction. Heat of a reaction can be determined by the following equation:
    q = mc\Delta T
    \(q\) = heat added \(m\) = mass of the substance \(c\) = specific heat \(\Delta T\) = change in temperature
  • Using stoichiometry, you can find the moles of product formed. Dividing q by that will yield the change in enthalpy of the reaction.

Heating Curves:

  • A heating curve shows what happens to the temperature of a substance as heat is added. The amount of the temperature increase is found through calorimetry. If the substance is in the process of a phase change, temperature is constant. The amount of heat needed to go through this phase is found through the heat of fusion or vaporization.
