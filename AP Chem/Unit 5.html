Rate Law:

    Using Initial Concentrations:
  • We want to measure the speed of a reaction, and we do this by quantifying how fast the reactants of a reaction disappear and how fast the products appear. We can quantify the change in reactants by seeing how fast the concentration changes.
  • The concentration of an element A is denoted as [A]
  • We define our rate to be:
    • average rate =
    Δconcentration
    Δtime
    =
    Δ[A]
    Δt
  • The rate is measured in Molarity/Molars (M) per second (s), so M/s, not to be confused with the units for velocity in physics m/s!
  • The rate of disappearance of the reactants is equal and opposite to the rate of appearance of the products in a reaction.
  • When we have the rate of one term in a chemical equation, we can find the rates for all the other terms based on their mole ratios.
  • Looking at the reactants of a reaction, we can create a general rate law:
    R = k[A]x[B]y[C]z
    where k is the rate constant, and x, y, and z are the orders of their respective reactants.
  • In the above equation, feel free to add more elements and orders as seen fit for the equation.
  • The rate constant is usually constant, but it does depend on temperature. The units for the rate constant differ based on reaction. For zero order reactions, it is in M/s, in first order reactions it is in 1/s, in second order reactions it is in 1/Ms and so forth, following the format of M/s * 1/M^order
  • Reactants in a reaction have "orders" which affect how much the rate changes when the concentration is changed. For zero order reactions, the rate is kept constant no matter how much the concentration changes.
  • The overall order of the reaction is the sum of all the orders.
