Equilibrium in Reversible Reactions:

  • In this unit, the focus is reversible reactions, reactions with low enough energy differences such that they can work both forwards and backwards.
The Equilibrium Constant:
  • Equilibrium occurs when in a reversible reaction, the rate of reactants becoming products is equivalent to the rate of products becoming reactants. However, just because the rates are the same, does NOT mean the concentrations are the same.
  • For a reaction {aA + bB ⇌ cC + dD}, the equilibrium constant is given by:
    • keq =
    [C]c[D]d
    [A]a[B]b
  • If keq < 1, the reaction favors the backwards direction since there is a higher concentration of reactants.
  • If keq > 1, the reaction favors the forwards direction since there is a higher concentration of products.
  • Reversible reactions at equilibrium with different states of matter are said to be in Heterogeneous Equilibrium. In these cases we ignore the solvents and the solids because they are in excess, so it makes sense to not take into account such high concentrations into our formula for the equilibrium constant.
  • In equilibrium reactions, pay attention to the state of matter. Concentrations of solids and liquids are omitted from all calculations. Another way to think of this is that liquids and solids don't have concentrations to begin with, there is no such thing as stronger water.
Le Châtelier's Principle:
  • Simply put, Le Châtelier's Principle tells us that when we "stress" out one side of a reversible reaction, the reaction will favor reaching equilibrium by going in the direction of the other side.
  • If we add more of one reactant, there is a higher probability of it colliding with other reactants, so other reactants would be used up more, and the forward reaction rate increases. When that happens, more products are created, and a new equilibrium will be reached, favoring the forward direction.
  • When the pressure is increased, the molecules are closer together, and have a higher probability of colliding. When pressure increases, the reaction favors the side with less molecules because it is easier to compress fewer molecules (think PV = nRT, as pressure goes up, moles goes down to keep it the same).
Changes In The Equilibrium Constant:
The Reaction Quotient:
  • The reaction quotient is the same thing as the equilibrium constant, except the usage varies. While kc gives us information when the reaction is at equilibrium, Qc can be calculated at any time.
    • Qc =
    [C]c[D]d
    [A]a[B]b
  • The reaction quotient can vary [0, ∞), if it is 0, the reaction is all reactants, and if it is ∞, the reaction is all products.
  • When the reaction is at equilibrium, Qc = Kc.
  • When Qc > Kc, that means the reaction wants to get closer to equilibrium, by Le Châtelier's Principle, so it would favor the reactants.
  • When Qc < Kc, the reaction favors the products.
  • For gaseous reactions, we can also express the equilibrium constant in terms of the partial pressures (Kp):
    Kp =
    (PC)c(PD)d
    (PA)a(PB)b
    where Δn is the difference in moles between the products and the reactants.
  • It differs from Kc by:
    • Kp = Kc(RT)Δn Show Proof

Small x approximation:
  • This method works best when it strongly favors one side of the reaction, either kc ≤ 10−4 or kc ≥ 104. If it is not, use the quadratic method.
  • You want to assume that the reaction is completely on one side or the other, depending on k
  • We make an ICE table for all the contents in the reaction, here's an example ICE table for a reaction {aA + bB → cC} that is going forward (Qc < Kc):
    • aA bB → cC
    Initial Initial Concentration of A Initial Concentration Of B Initial Concentration of C
    Change −ax −bx +cx
    Concentration at Equilibrium Initial + Change Initial + Change Initial + Change
  • You want to pick coefficients to your change variables based on the term of the balanced equation with the lowest stoichiometric coefficient.
  • Make sure all the units are the same, like in Molars, Partial Pressures, or amounts.
  • Once we find the Equilibrium Concentrations, we can use that to calculate Kc.

Solubility:

  • When you dissolve salt into water, you are putting the solute NaCl into the solvent H2O through the process of hydration.
  • The water molecules are able to pull apart the ionic bond and create Na^+ and Cl^- ions because water molecules are polar dipoles. They attract the ions and surround them in the form of an ion-dipole interaction.
  • This process is also known as dissolution, when a solid is made into a solution. The opposite of this would be precipitation, where ions in a solution form a solid.
  • For weak acids/bases and other slightly soluble molecules, we like to measure just how much is actually dissolved. We can measure the solubility based on how many grams are dissolved per liter. We can also convert this to Molars (mol/L) for easier use with our other equations.
  • We represent the dissolution of weak acids/bases and other slightly soluble molecules with reversible reactions, so naturally, they will reach an equilibrium when that certain amount dissolves. We can solve for an equilibrium constant for the solubility reaction Ksp, we call this the solubility product constant.
  • We can find Ksp by setting up an ICE table, and allowing our found solubility molar to be the change in the reactant's molarity. From there, and assuming the reaction has just started, we can fill in the rest of the table to solve for K sp. Since we don't take into account solids in equilibrium reactions, we only need the concentrations of the products. For a reaction
    MxAy ⇌ xMx^+ + yAy^-, we can find the solubility product constant:
    • Ksp = [My^+]x[Ax^-]y

  • The common-ion effect is when you try to dissolve a molecule into a solution that already has one of the ions, like PbCl2 into KCl, since there are already Cl^- ions in the solution, less of the PbCl2 will dissolve into the solution. We can back this up with Le Châtelier's Principle, since we are adding more "stress" to the products side with the extra Cl^-, the reversible solubility reaction for PbCl2 will shift more towards the reactants, thus having a lower molar solubility in KCl as opposed to just pure water.
  • We can use the common-ion effect to separate out a solid that is slightly soluble. By adding in more of a common-ion, we can make more of the solid by stressing the products side of the reversible solubility reaction.
