Polarity:

  • Molecules are nonpolar when all of the bond dipoles within the molecule cancel each other out
  • Molecular are considered polar when all of the bond dipoles within the molecule do NOT cancel each other out
  • One way to see if a molecule is polar is to see if you can easily separate all the different types with one line. If so, then it is polar, but if everything is evenly distributed, then it must be nonpolar. Pay attention to the partial charges denoted by δ+ and δ−

Intermolecular Forces (IMF's):

  • InterMolecular Forces (abbreviated IMF's) are the forces that hold together other molecules, and they are considered pretty weak in comparison to Intramolecular Forces, which are the forces that hold the atoms together within a single molecule.
  • London Dispersion Forces are the forces formed when nonpolar molecules are polarized by other nonpolar molecules temporarily, distorting the electron clouds in the right manner causing an even weaker force of attraction. We say that the more electrons these molecules have, with more ease can they be polarized, creating stronger London Dispersion Forces.
  • Dipole-Dipole Forces are forces that hold polar molecules together, unlike the London Dispersion Forces. The attraction happens between a partially positive end of one molecule, and a partially negative end of another.
  • With Dipole-Induced Dipole Forces, this is when a polar molecule temporarily polarizes a non-polar molecule and there is an attraction caused between them.
  • Hydrogen Bonding Attractions are strong dipole-dipole interactions, which occur between an H atom on one molecule and a N, O, or F on another molecule.
  • Ion-Dipole Forces are attractive force between ions and polar molecules.

Vapor Pressure:

  • Vapor Pressure is the pressure exerted by a gas in equilibrium with its liquid phase at a given temperature. Temperature and vapor pressure are proportional.
  • At the same temperature, stronger IMF's mean that molecules are less likely to break their IMF's and escape to form a gas, therefore, higher IMF's means lower vapor pressures.

Kinetic Molecular Theory (KMT):

  • We normalize all gases to an "ideal gas" that closely resembles the behavior of real gases without the minor intricacies.
  • Gases are composed of molecules that are in continuous motion, traveling in straight lines and changing direction only when they collide with other molecules or the walls of the container
  • The pressure exerted by a gas in a container results from collisions between the gas molecules and the container walls
  • The average kinetic energy of the gas molecules that is proportional to the kelvin temperature of the gas
  • The molecules composing the gas are negligibly small compared to the distances between them (negligible volume)
  • Gas molecules exert no attractive or repulsive forces on each other or the container walls as we assume them to be nonpolar.

    Dalton's Law:
  • Dalton's Law of Partial Pressures simply states that the total pressure of multiple gases mixed together is simply the sum of all the pressures of each individual gas as the pressure exerted by each component is independent of the other components.
    • Deviations from Ideal Behavior:
    Ideal Gases: Real Gases:
    We assume the volume to be negligible In reality gas particles do have volume, and with low temperatures and high pressures the volume is no longer negligible in comparison to that of the container
    We assume that gas molecules have no attraction/repulsion forces with other gas molecules In reality gas particles do have attractive/repulsive tendencies with other molecules, the attractive or repulsive forces can impact the strengths of the collisions.

Electromagnetic Spectrum:

  • Light is a form of electromagnetic radiation, so it travels in waves.
  • The frequency of a light wave is given by f, the number of waves passing in one second, or Hertz (Hz)
    • v = λf
  • In the case of light through a vacuum, the velocity v would actually be the speed of light in a vacuum c = 2.998 × 108. Note: all other types of electromagnetic radiation (microwaves, radio waves) have the same speed.

Beer's Law:

  • Using spectrophotometry to measure the absorbance of certain wavelengths of light, we can find the concentration of a compound within a solution, this is given by the Beer-Lambert Law:
    A = εLC
    where A is the absorbance, ε is the molar absorptivity, L is the length of the light path, and C is the concentration of the compound.
