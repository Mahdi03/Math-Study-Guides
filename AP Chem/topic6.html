The Kinetic Molecular Theory:

  • We normalize all gases to an "ideal gas" that closely resembles the behavior of real gases without the minor intricacies.
  • Gases are composed of molecules that are in continuous motion, traveling in straight lines and changing direction only when they collide with other molecules or the walls of the container
  • The pressure exerted by a gas in a container results from collisions between the gas molecules and the container walls
  • The average kinetic energy of the gas molecules that is proportional to the kelvin temperature of the gas
  • The molecules composing the gas are negligibly small compared to the distances between them (negligible volume)
  • Gas molecules exert no attractive or repulsive forces on each other or the container walls as we assume them to be nonpolar.

  • Dalton's Law of Partial Pressures simply states that the total pressure of multiple gases mixed together is simply the sum of all the pressures of each individual gas as the pressure exerted by each component is independent of the other components.
  • Differences between an ideal gas and a real gas:
    • Ideal Gases: Real Gases:
    We assume the volume to be negligible In reality gas particles do have volume, and with low temperatures and high pressures the volume is no longer negligible in comparison to that of the container
    We assume that gas molecules have no attraction/repulsion forces with other gas molecules In reality gas particles do have attractive/repulsive tendencies with other molecules, the attractive or repulsive forces can impact the strengths of the collisions.
