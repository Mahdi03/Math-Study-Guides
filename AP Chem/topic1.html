  • The mass of an individual unit is measured in Atomic Mass Units, or amu for short. We say that one oxygen atom is 16.00 amu whereas one mole of oxygen is 16.00 grams.

Mass Spectroscopy of Elements:

  • The mass spectrum of a sample containing of a single element can be used to determine the identity of the isotopes of that element and the relative abundance of each isotope in nature.
  • Simply put, we can average all the atomic masses of an element to find the real atomic weight.
    Take Carbon for example, we have 98% C-12 and 2% C-13, so the real atomic weight would be (98%)(12) + (2%)(13), and this applies with elements that come in more than two masses as well.

Elemental Composition of Pure Substances:

  • Compounds are either made up of molecules or they are empirical formulas, which have the lowest whole number ratio of atoms of the elements in a compound. An example would be C6H12O6 (a molecular formula) versus CH2O (an empirical formula).
  • To find the empirical formula, find the moles of each element in a compound, and then divide them all by the smallest number of moles. Although they may not be whole numbers, do your best to round, unless you clearly have a decimal like 0.5 or 0.3, in which case you would need to multiply all those values by a certain factor to get them as close to whole numbers as possible.   • The Law of Definite Proportions says that the ratio of mass of the constituent elements in any pure sample of that compound is always the same.

Composition of Mixtures:

  • Mixtures contain molecules or empirical formulas of two or more types in varying proportions. Elemental analysis can be used to determine the relative numbers of atoms in a substance and to determine its purity.
