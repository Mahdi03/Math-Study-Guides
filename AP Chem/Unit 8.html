pH:

  • pH is just a mathematical abbreviation describing the concentration of H^+ ions in a solution
  • Likewise, pOH is a mathematical abbreviation describing the concentration of OH^- ions in a solution
  • The formulas below give the actual longhand for the mathematics behind acidity concentrations:
    • pH = [H^+] [H^+] = 10−pH
    pOH = − [OH^-] [OH^-] = 10−pOH
  • So as you can see, the "p" is shorthand for the −log of something.
  • While H+ ions make acids, OH- ions make bases
  • For aqueous solutions at 25°:
    • pH + pOH = 14
  • However, pH isn't always a measurement of the strength of acidity, for instance, while strong acids completely disassociate into H+ ions, weak acids of the same concentration won't, thus having a lesser pH

Acid Strengths:

  • There are strong acids and weak acids.
  • Strong acids completely disassociate in a solution and thusly have more H^+ ions freely moving about. Their disassociation reactions are one-way since they are able to completely separate. With a higher concentration of H^+ ions, their pH is lower, making them stronger acids.
  • Weak acids aren't able to completely disassociate in a solution, so they have some molecules floating around, and some ions floating around. Because they don't disassociate completely, their disassociation reactions are reversible. They have a lower concentration of H^+ ions floating around, giving them a higher pH and an overall lower acidity.
  • We can see how much an acid disassociates through the acid disassociation constant Ka, which is just the equilibrium constant of the disassociation reaction (minus the water of course since that is an abundant reactant). The higher the Ka value, the more the acid disassociates (since it leans more towards products).
  • The lower the pKa value, the stronger the acid (p represents a negative logarithmic graph, so as input increases, the output decreases). The stronger the acid, the more stable its conjugate base.
  • The percent disassociation is the concentration of the acidic/basic component of the compound over the concentration of the entire compound.
  • If we are given the ka value as well as the initial concentrations, we can use the small x approximation methods with ICE tables to find the equilibrium concentrations, and then use those concentrations to find the pH or pOH of the solution.
  • With bases, the same thing applies, but this time with a base disassociation constant kb even though bases don't disassociate, rather, they ionize.
  • The same concepts apply, and at 25°C kb is related to ka by:
    • pka + pkb = 14 ka × kb = kw
  • We get the second equation from the first using log properties, and kw is the autoionization constant for water.
  • Autoionization of water is when water molecules react with themselves to form slightly acidic and slightly basic molecules from time to time, this reaction is reversible. The reaction looks like:
    • H2O + H2O ⇌ H3O^+ + OH^- kw = [ H3O^+][ OH^-] = 10−14 at 25°C

Neutralization Reactions:

Buffers:

  • Buffer solutions resist change to strong acids and bases, minimizing the effect on the pH/pOH.
  • Let's say we have a dissolution reaction of a weak acid. If we add a molecule into the solution such that it creates another source for those H^+ ions, then by Le Châtelier's Principle, that added molecule acts as a buffer, since it adds stress to the products side of the dissolution reaction.
  • But even at one point buffers can't withstand pH changes forever, they reach a point called the buffering capacity where the buffer can no longer neutralize the solution, which can be found from titration.

Indicators:

Titration:

  • A titration is an experiment that can be used to calculate the amount of moles or the concentration of one chemical by reacting it with a known quantity of another chemical.
  • The standard solution of known concentration is known as the titrant and the solution of unknown concentration is known as the analyte.
  • The titrant is added slowly to the analyte by using a buret which drops it into a flask.
  • The equivalence point in a titration is the point at which just enough titrant has been added to react with all of the analyte. This point may be indicated by a color change in the flask.
