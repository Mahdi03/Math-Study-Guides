Chemical Bonds:

  • Ionic Bonding: A solid that doesn’t conduct electricity, but becomes a conductor when melted or dissolved in water.
  • Covalent Molecular: A solid that doesn’t conduct electricity, as a solid, liquid, or aqueous solution. They tend to have a low melting point.
  • Covalent Network: A solid that doesn’t conduct electricity as a solid, liquid, or aqueous solution. They tend to have a high melting point
  • Metallic Bonding: A solid that is a good conductor of electricity. At the particle level, a metal consists of a crystal lattice of positive particles that consist of the nucleus and the core electrons, with a “sea” of delocalized valence electrons that can move freely throughout the lattice

Chemical Bond Strength:

  • The longer the bond, the easier it is to break (single bonds are longer than double bond)
  • Polar bonds are weaker than nonpolar bonds
  • Double bonds are stronger than single bonds (pi bonds > sigma bonds)

Alloys:

  • An interstitial alloy is formed between two elements in which there is a significant difference between two atomic radius of each element (steel)
  • A substitutional alloy is formed between two elements in which the atomic radius of each element is very similar; atoms of one elements are substituted for the other (brass)
