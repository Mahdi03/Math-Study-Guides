6.1: Cauchy-Euler Equations:

  • So far we have only focused on differential equations with constant coefficients, but now it's time to focus on non-constant coefficients. Cauchy-Euler Equations are differential equations of the form:
    • anxn + an − 1xn − 1 + ... + a1x + a0y = 0
  • We can suspect the solutions to be of the form y = xn, and by plugging it in to the equation, we start to see that when the substituted equation has n distinct roots, the general solution is:
    • y(x) = c1xm1 + c2xm2 + ... + cnxmn
  • When we have repeating roots, we multiply subsequent terms by another x. The set of solutions for a root of multiplicity k is:
    • {x, x x, x[ x]2, ..., x[ x]k − 1}
  • When we have complex roots, we solve for the roots m, put them back into y = em, and then using Euler's Identity (eiθ = θ + i θ) and the Superposition Principle to make our answer real. When the root is m = a ± bi, the general solution will be:
    • y(x) = c1xa b x + c2xa b x

    6.2: Power Series Solutions to Differential Equations:

  • When we have Cauchy-Euler Differential Equations with polynomial coefficients, we assume the solution would be a polynomial of unknown degree. We can represent that polynomial in the form of a power series.
  • We assume the polynomial to be the form of:
    • cnxn
  • In order to test a power series solution, we write the probable power series solution in summation notation and substitute back into the equation.
  • When we differentiate a summation, the index increases by one to account for the lost term.
  • When the index changes, we need to equalize the indexes at some other variable so that we can manipulate the multiple summations.
  • Once you find a power series solution, don't forget to find the interval of convergence. Recall the Ratio Test from Unit 4 in the AP Calculus BC Study Guide.

    • 6.3: Solutions About Ordinary Points:

  • After normalizing a differential equation with polynomial coefficients, we get an equation of the form:
    • y'' + P(x)y' + Q(x)y = 0
  • In this scenario, P(x) and Q(x) need to be able to expressed in the form of a power series. We say these equations are analytic at their centers x = xc.
  • For the differential equation, we say the center to those functions P(x) and Q(x) is considered an ordinary point.
