  • Objects such as springs do not move in a linear path, instead, they move in a back and forth path continuously, they exhibit forms of Simple Harmonic Motion.
  • All this is, is when an object oscillates back and forth uninterrupted as the system tries to restore itself to equilibrium (the center point where the system no longer moves, or when displacement is 0), such that when its motion is graphed on a line, it produces a sinusoidal function (it moves like a sine graph).
  • We can (and shortly will) prove that simple harmonic motion is sinusoidal.
  • Springs have a Spring Force Constant ( k) that varies from spring to spring.

    • 5.2: Springs with Dampers:

  • The previous section is heavily physics-related. Naturally, we assumed there was no friction to keep the model simpler, but in reality there is friction, and with friction we have Simple Harmonic Motion that slowly decays.
  • In these kinds of situations we say that the spring exhibits damped motion.
  • Originally we had a differential equation in the form of m + kx = 0. But now we have to account for damping over time. We modify our differential equation to include a damping constant ( β)
    • m + β + kx = 0
  • Once we convert into the auxiliary equation and solve using the quadratic formula, there are three possibilities with the discriminant ( β2 − 4mk):
    1. Discriminant > 0:

      We get 1 real solution, and we say that the system is critically damped

    5.3: Forced Motion:

  • Building off of our last model ( m = −β − kx ), sometimes there are scenarios where there is a mechanical force that also affects our system. We call this the forcing function, as denoted by f (x) in m = −β − kx + f(t). When we rewrite this as follows, we start to see a difference.
    • m + β + kx = f(t)
  • While Damped Motion dealt with HOLDEN's, Forced Motion deals with NOLDEN's. Other than that, we already know how to solve these kinds of problems using the annihilator and what not.
  • Sometimes the particular solution might not match up with sinusoidal behavior, but if the limit at t → ∞ cancels out a part of the solution, we call it the transient solution, but if the part of the solution remains, we call it the steady-state solution.
