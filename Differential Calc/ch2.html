Beginnings of Differential Equations:

  • Differential equations are equations that have derivatives of nay equation of any order in them. They can come in the form of ordinary differential equations (ODE), equations that have one dependent variable and derivatives with respect to one variable, and partial differential equations (PDE), equations that have more than one dependent variable and derivatives with respect to multiple variables and partial derivatives. For the sake of this class we are only dealing with ODE's.
  • We can use differential equations to solve certain rate problems, such as when given the initial volume and concentration of liquid in a tank, the rate and concentration at which liquid enters the tank, and the rate at which liquid leaves the tank, you can set up a differential equation (rate in − rate out)
  • Linear differential equations come in the form:

  • To verify a given solution to the differential equation, differentiate the potential solution as many times as needed to plug it in to the differential equation and check to see if it satisfies the criteria.
  • If y1 and y2 are solutions to a linear differential equation, then any linear combination c1y1(x) + c2y1(x) is also a solution to the differential equation.

    • 2.2: Separable Differential Equations:

  • A differential equation is separable when you can move all of the terms of one variable to one side and all of the terms of the other variable to the other side. This would mean splitting the derivative sign by taking advantage of the differential. Once what you have separated the differential equation, you can integrate both sides. Just remember to add a constant C to only ONE side of the equation.
  • If you'd like, you are allowed to turn ec = A for easier algebraic manipulation purposes. Remember, the end goal is to solve for an equation.
  • You can apply EUST to this as well.
  • When your family of functions found from separating the differential equation does not coincide with the given initial condition, That initial condition is called a singular solution.
  • If EUST does not apply, then separation of variables does not necessarily guarantee that you have found all of the solutions to the differential equation.

    2.6: Bernoulli and Ricotti Differential Equations:

    Bernoulli Equations:
  • Bernoulli differential equations are very similar to FOLDE's, the only difference being an extra power of y on the right-hand side:
    • + P(x)y = f (x)yn
  • Solve these differential equations simply divide both sides by yn to make a FOLDE, and then substitute a value to form the differential equation into a FOLDE.
    • Ricotti Equations:
  • In a similar succession, Ricotti equations are almost like Bernoulli equations:
    • = P(x) + Q(x)y + R(x)y2
  • With these equations, one solution to the differential equation must already be provided so that you can use a first solution y1 and substitution through differentiating:
    • y = y1 + u
